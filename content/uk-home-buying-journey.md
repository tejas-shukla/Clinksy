# The UK Home Buying Journey — 10 stages

Clinkeys's knowledge base for the AI assistant and for future deep-dive guide pages. Covers England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. Scotland is different (separate Missives process, LBTT, no exchange/completion split) — handled in its own guide.

**Typical total timescale:** 12 weeks to 8 months end-to-end. Highly dependent on the chain and the solicitor.

**The four marketing phases on the landing page map to these stages like this:**
- Get ready → Stage 1
- Find your home → Stages 2–3
- Make it official → Stages 4–8
- Get the keys → Stages 9–10

---

## Stage 1 — Work out how much you can borrow

**Timescale:** Up to a week.

**What happens:** Before you start house-hunting, you understand two numbers: how much cash you have for the upfront costs, and how much a lender will let you borrow on top. Most buyers get a mortgage agreement in principle (AIP) at this stage — a soft-credit-check letter that says "we'd probably lend you up to £X." Valid for 30 or 90 days depending on the lender.

**Typical cost:** Free. Brokers usually don't charge first-time buyers.

**Common gotchas:**
- Credit-card balances and car finance reduce how much a lender will offer.
- A thin credit file (no cards, no history) can be as damaging as a bad one.
- Treating the AIP figure as your "budget" — it's the ceiling, not the comfortable number.

**Questions to ask a broker:**
- Are you whole-of-market or tied to specific lenders?
- How is your fee structured, and when is it payable?
- Which lenders are friendliest to my situation (self-employed, contract, recent UK arrival)?

## Stage 2 — Find your dream home

**Timescale:** Two weeks to six months or longer.

**What happens:** You search Rightmove, Zoopla, OnTheMarket, PrimeLocation; you visit properties; you learn what your budget actually buys in the areas you're considering. If you're selling at the same time, prioritise getting your own offer accepted *before* you start viewing — most sellers won't take you seriously otherwise.

**Typical cost:** £0 plus your time and travel.

**Common gotchas:**
- Falling in love before you've checked the leasehold terms, EPC rating, or actual broadband speed.
- Ignoring tenure — leasehold flats often carry ground rent and service charges (£200+/month).
- Missing the area: visit at different times of day, weekday and weekend.

**Questions to ask the estate agent:**
- Why is the seller moving? How long has it been on the market?
- Is it freehold or leasehold? If leasehold, how many years remain and what's the service charge?
- Are there any other offers on the table? What's the seller's chain situation?

---

## Stage 3 — Make an offer

**Timescale:** A day to two weeks.

**What happens:** You tell the estate agent (verbally or in writing) what you'd like to pay. They take it to the seller. You may go back and forth a few times. Once accepted, the property is marked "Sold STC" (Subject to Contract).

**Typical cost:** £0 to make an offer.

**Common gotchas:**
- Bidding without knowing comparable sale prices in the postcode — Land Registry data is free.
- Forgetting to ask for fixtures and fittings (curtains, white goods, sheds) to be included.
- Anti-money-laundering checks: most estate agents now charge buyers £20–£60 for AML ID checks. Annoying, but unavoidable.

**Questions to think through before offering:**
- What's my walk-away number?
- What recent comparables exist for this street/postcode?
- What conditions am I attaching (subject to survey, no onward chain, fixtures included)?

> 🥂 **Milestone: Offer accepted.** You are NOT legally bound yet. Until contracts exchange (Stage 8), either side can pull out. Speed matters from here.
>
> - **Gazumping:** another buyer offers more and the seller switches to them. You can ask the seller to "take it off the market" as a condition of your offer.
> - **Gazanging:** the seller decides to stay (often because prices are rising). Almost nothing you can do.

---

## Stage 4 — Secure a mortgage offer

**Timescale:** Two to eight weeks.

**What happens:** Your AIP from Stage 1 may have expired by now. Either way, you formally apply with the lender (or your broker submits on your behalf). The lender does full underwriting — proper credit check, income verification, valuation of the property — and issues a formal mortgage offer. Usually valid for 3–6 months. Tip: don't automatically go back to the same lender — the market changes daily, and a 1% difference on £150k over 25 years is roughly £26k.

**Typical cost:** Arrangement fees range from £0 to £1,999 depending on the product. Booking and valuation fees may apply separately.

**Common gotchas:**
- Changing jobs, opening new credit, or making large purchases between offer and approval can cause the lender to reassess.
- A "downvaluation" — the lender's surveyor values the property below the agreed price — forces renegotiation or extra deposit.
- Letting the offer expire before completion. Reapplying may now be at worse rates.

**Questions to confirm:**
- Are there any conditions on this offer I need to satisfy?
- What's the expiry date and what happens if we don't complete in time?
- Is there an early repayment charge if I want to overpay or remortgage early?

> 🥂 **Milestone: Mortgage offer received.** Cross-check the offer document against your earlier mortgage illustration. Names spelt correctly? Figures right? Any discrepancy goes straight back to the broker — even a misspelt name can cause delays or, worst case, withdrawal of the offer.

## Stage 5 — Kick-start the legal process

**Timescale:** Eight to twelve weeks (runs in parallel with stages 4 and 6).

**What happens:** You appoint a conveyancer or licensed solicitor to handle the legal transfer. They draft contracts, run local-authority searches (planning, drainage, environmental), check the title, and eventually handle the money. If you're taking out a mortgage, your conveyancer must be on your lender's panel — confirm before you instruct them.

**Typical cost:** £1,000–£2,500 including disbursements (searches, Land Registry, bank transfer fees). Higher in London and for leasehold purchases.

**Common gotchas:**
- Choosing the cheapest quote — slow conveyancing kills chains.
- Hidden "no completion, no fee" terms that aren't actually no-fee.
- Not chasing. Most delays are recovered by polite, persistent phone calls. If a solicitor is the bottleneck, escalate in writing to a senior partner.

**Questions to ask a conveyancer:**
- Are you on my lender's panel?
- What's your average time from instruction to exchange?
- What's included in your fixed fee, and what's billed separately?

---

## Stage 6 — Sort a property survey

**Timescale:** Two to four weeks.

**What happens:** The lender's valuation is NOT a survey — it's a basic check that the property exists and is worth roughly what you're paying. You commission a proper survey separately to check the actual condition. Usually done after the mortgage offer comes through (no point paying for a survey on a deal that might not happen) but always before exchange.

**The three main types:**
- **Level 1 — Snagging survey.** New builds only. £0 if you self-survey with a free checklist, or £400–£600 from a professional firm.
- **Level 2 — HomeBuyer report.** Conventional homes less than 50 years old. £500–£1,000.
- **Level 3 — Full structural survey.** Older or unusual properties. Up to £1,500. Belt-and-braces detail — and ammunition to renegotiate the price.

**Typical cost:** £400–£1,500 depending on level and property size.

**Common gotchas:**
- Confusing the lender's valuation with a survey.
- Skipping the survey on a 100-year-old house to save money — and discovering damp or structural issues later.
- Not using survey findings to renegotiate. If problems are flagged, get a builder's quote for repairs and ask the seller to either fix or knock the cost off.

**Questions to ask the surveyor:**
- What's the difference between Level 2 and Level 3 for *this* property?
- Will the report include a renovation or repair cost estimate?
- How quickly can you turn it around?

## Stage 7 — Arrange buildings insurance

**Timescale:** Up to a week.

**What happens:** You compare buildings insurance quotes and line up a policy that starts the moment contracts exchange. Once you exchange (Stage 8), you're legally bound to buy — and from that moment you carry the risk if the building burns down before completion. So insurance needs to be live from exchange day, not move-in day.

**Typical cost:** Varies hugely. £150–£500/year is typical for a flat or small house. Use the rebuild value from the lender's valuation as your sum insured (not the purchase price).

**Common gotchas:**
- Setting the start date to completion instead of exchange. A gap means uninsured liability.
- Insuring for the purchase price rather than the rebuild cost.
- Auto-renewing year after year without checking the market. New customer deals are usually 20–40% cheaper.

**Questions to confirm:**
- Does the policy start on or before our exchange date?
- Does it cover accidental damage and alternative accommodation if the house becomes uninhabitable?
- What's the excess on subsidence and on escape of water (the two biggest claim types)?

---

## Stage 8 — Get ready to exchange contracts

**Timescale:** Two to three weeks.

**What happens:** Your solicitor walks you through the local-authority search results and any enquiries that came up. There may be back-and-forth on issues like planning permissions or covenants. You agree a completion date with the seller (and anyone else in the chain). You sign the contract and send your deposit to your solicitor — usually 10% of the purchase price.

**Typical cost:** Your deposit (e.g. £35,000 on a £350k home). Plus £20–£35 per CHAPS payment if you're moving more than your bank's daily limit (£25,000 at most banks).

**Common gotchas:**
- Buildings insurance start date — see Stage 7.
- Last-minute chain problems (someone's mortgage falls through, someone pulls out).
- Underestimating that the completion date is contractually fixed once you exchange.

**Questions to confirm with your solicitor:**
- Has my buildings insurance start date been set to today?
- What's the exact completion date and what time does it take effect?
- What happens if either side fails to complete on the agreed date?

> 🥂 **Milestone: Contracts exchanged.** Solicitors swap signed contracts. **Legally binding** from this moment. Pulling out now means forfeiting your deposit (10%) — but the seller can't back out either.

---

## Stage 9 — Prepare for completion and move in

**Timescale:** Two to five weeks (from exchange to completion).

**What happens:** Your solicitor produces a completion statement — a clear breakdown of every penny you owe (remaining deposit, Stamp Duty, legal fees, disbursements). You sign the transfer deed in front of a witness. On the day, your solicitor draws down the mortgage funds, sends the full payment to the seller's solicitor, receives the title deeds in return. Once the money lands, you officially own the property and the estate agent releases the keys (typically around midday).

**Typical cost:** Final balance to complete. SDLT due within 14 days — see Stage 10.

**Common gotchas:**
- Bank transfer cut-off times. Money sent late means keys in the afternoon — or not at all that day.
- Forgetting to do a final viewing the day before completion to check fixtures and fittings are as agreed.
- Missing meter readings on move-in day. Photograph everything.

**Questions to handle on completion day:**
- Have I read the meters and taken photos?
- Have I confirmed all fixtures and fittings are present per the contract?
- Do I have the keys to *every* door, plus alarm codes and Wi-Fi details?

> 🥂 **Milestone: Completion. You own a home.**

---

## Stage 10 — Complete the final steps

**Timescale:** Instantly to two weeks.

**What happens:** Three tidy-up tasks, mostly handled by your solicitor:

1. **Pay Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT).** Within 14 days of completion. First-time buyers in England/NI pay 0% on the first £425,000 (up to a max property price of £625,000). Standard rates apply above that. Wales (LTT) and Scotland (LBTT) have their own scales.
2. **Officially register your ownership.** Your solicitor sends details to HM Land Registry. Registration fee is £250–£500 depending on price.
3. **Get the title deeds.** Your solicitor forwards them to your mortgage lender (or to you if you bought outright). Many people leave them on file with the solicitor.

**Typical cost:** SDLT (depends on price and buyer status — use a calculator). Land Registry fee £250–£500. Removals £400–£1,500 depending on distance and volume.

**Common gotchas:**
- Forgetting to redirect post (Royal Mail redirection, ~£35 for six months).
- Missing the 14-day SDLT window.
- Discovering on day three that something the surveyor flagged is broken — but not having the receipts to follow up.

**Questions to handle in week one:**
- Have I changed my address with: banks, employer, DVLA, GP, electoral roll?
- Have I taken meter readings and submitted them to the new utility providers?
- Do I have my buildings insurance documents accessible?
